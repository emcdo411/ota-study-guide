# Domains 5 & 6: Clinical Reasoning + Psychosocial Practice

---

## DOMAIN 5: CLINICAL REASONING & PROBLEM-SOLVING

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **Clinical reasoning**: The cognitive process OTAs use to make decisions about intervention, prioritization, and communication with the supervising OT
- **Narrative reasoning**: Understanding the client's story, their occupational history, and what matters to them
- **Procedural reasoning**: "What do I do next?" — knowledge-driven, step-by-step clinical thinking
- **Interactive reasoning**: Real-time relational adjustments during a session based on client cues
- **Conditional reasoning**: Forward thinking — "If this intervention works, where will the client be in 3 months?"
- **Pragmatic reasoning**: Realistic thinking — considering setting, reimbursement, time, and available resources
- **Problem-solving within supervision**: OTAs bring concerns, observations, and data to the OT; they do not independently revise the treatment plan

**Memorization Framework:**
**NIPCP** — The 5 Clinical Reasoning Modes
- **N**arrative (the client's story)
- **I**nteractive (in-the-moment responsiveness)
- **P**rocedural (what's the next step)
- **C**onditional (what's the future vision)
- **P**ragmatic (what's actually possible here)

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know which reasoning type matches the clinical scenario — narrative questions involve life story; procedural involves technique sequence
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] If a client is not responding to a treatment intervention, the OTA's correct action is to observe, document, and REPORT to supervising OT — not independently change the plan
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] Practice connecting reasoning modes to real treatment scenarios: "Client cries during dressing — which reasoning mode do you use?" (Interactive)

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ During a treatment session, a client who had been making progress suddenly refuses to participate and seems withdrawn. Walk through all 5 reasoning modes and describe what each would prompt you to think and do.

---

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain is about HOW OTAs think through problems — not just what they do. They use multiple mental frameworks simultaneously: understanding someone's story, responding in real time, following clinical protocols, and being realistic about what the setting allows. Good OTAs think, not just execute.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **situational leadership and adaptive decision-making under constraints**. Effective leaders don't apply one management style universally — they read the room (interactive), follow the playbook (procedural), keep the long view (conditional), and stay grounded in what's actually resourced (pragmatic). The best clinical reasoning mirrors the best operational thinking.

> 💡 Organizations that train for procedural compliance only — without developing adaptive reasoning — produce staff who follow scripts until the script runs out.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> An OTA applying clinical reasoning across five simultaneous modes — narrative, interactive, procedural, conditional, pragmatic — uses the same cognitive architecture as a senior executive navigating a crisis: context first, relationship second, protocol third, vision fourth, reality check throughout.

## DOMAIN 6: PSYCHOSOCIAL PRACTICE & MENTAL HEALTH

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **Therapeutic use of self**: The intentional use of one's personality, communication style, and therapeutic relationship to facilitate the client's progress
- **Mental health settings**: Acute psychiatric, community mental health, substance use recovery, school-based, forensic
- **Recovery model**: The principle that people with mental illness can live meaningful, self-directed lives — OT supports this through occupation
- **Psychosocial factors in physical care**: Even in acute care or rehab settings, depression, anxiety, and motivation affect occupational performance
- **Therapeutic relationship boundaries**: OTAs must maintain professional boundaries — no dual relationships, no self-disclosure beyond therapeutic purpose
- **Cognitive-Behavioral frame of reference**: Addresses thoughts, beliefs, and behaviors that affect daily function
- **MOHO (Model of Human Occupation)**: Theoretical model examining volition, habituation, performance capacity, and environment as factors in occupational participation

**Memorization Framework:**
**BRAVE-T** — Therapeutic Use of Self Core Skills
- **B**eing present (attentive, not distracted)
- **R**eflective listening (hear what's underneath)
- **A**uthenticity (genuine, not performative)
- **V**alidation (acknowledge the client's experience)
- **E**mpathy (not sympathy — feel WITH, not FOR)
- **T**herapeutic boundaries (always maintained)

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know MOHO's four concepts: Volition (motivation), Habituation (routines/roles), Performance Capacity (skills), Environment (physical/social context)
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] Psychosocial scenarios often test boundary maintenance — if a client offers a gift, the correct answer is almost always to decline with a therapeutic explanation
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] For any physical rehab case, identify at least one psychosocial barrier (e.g., depression post-stroke) and how it affects engagement — exams reward this integrative thinking

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ Using the MOHO model, analyze a client with schizophrenia who has stopped cooking for themselves. Address volition, habituation, performance capacity, and environment separately.

---

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain is about the human side of care — understanding that people's emotions, mental state, and motivation directly affect their ability to do anything, including get better. OTAs are trained to use the therapeutic relationship itself as a tool, and to work in settings where mental health is the primary focus.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **psychological safety and employee engagement science** applied at the individual level. Leaders who understand that performance is downstream of psychological state — that a demoralized team member can't execute regardless of training — are practicing the same wisdom OTAs apply in psychosocial care.

> 💡 The organization that invests in psychological safety doesn't just get happier employees — it gets higher performance, the same way psychosocial intervention doesn't just improve mood, it unlocks occupational function.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> An OTA's therapeutic use of self — intentionally leveraging empathy, presence, and relational skill to unlock a client's functional capacity — is the clinical parallel to a leader's investment in psychological safety: in both cases, the "soft" relational intervention is the direct mechanism for "hard" performance outcomes.
