# Domains 7 & 8: Assistive Technology + Documentation & Reimbursement

## DOMAIN 7: ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY & ADAPTIVE EQUIPMENT

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **Assistive Technology (AT)**: Any device, tool, or system that helps a person with a disability perform tasks they might otherwise struggle with or be unable to do
- **Low-tech AT**: Non-electronic, simple devices — built-up handles, button hooks, reacher grabbers, sock aids, rocker knives
- **High-tech AT**: Electronic/digital solutions — power wheelchairs, AAC devices, smart home controls, environmental control units
- **AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)**: Devices and strategies supporting communication for those with speech/language impairment
- **Universal Design**: Designing environments and products to be usable by all people, regardless of ability, without adaptation
- **Seating and positioning**: Proper body alignment in wheelchair or adaptive seating to prevent pressure injury and enable function
- **Environmental modification**: Changing the physical environment to support independence (grab bars, ramp installation, kitchen lowering)
- **Funding pathways**: Medicaid, Medicare, vocational rehabilitation, private insurance — OTAs must understand documentation requirements for AT funding

**Memorization Framework:**
**LAST-F** — AT Recommendation Framework
- **L**ow-tech first (simplest solution that works)
- **A**daptation of task or environment
- **S**eating and positioning (prerequisite to function)
- **T**echnology match to client goals and capacity
- **F**unding pathway identified before recommendation finalized

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know which AT is appropriate for specific diagnoses — ALS/AAC, SCI/power mobility, stroke/one-handed techniques, low vision/optical magnifiers
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] AT scenarios often ask what you recommend FIRST — always low-tech before high-tech unless the clinical picture clearly requires it
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] Practice training scenarios: know HOW to teach a client to use a reacher, install a grab bar correctly, or set up a one-handed cutting board

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ Your client has C6 spinal cord injury with preserved wrist extension. What functional movements do they have? What low-tech and high-tech AT would you recommend to support independence in dressing and meal preparation?

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain covers the tools and technology OTAs use to help clients do things independently — from a simple jar opener to a voice-controlled home system. The job includes matching the right tool to the right person AND knowing how to get it funded through insurance or government programs.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **technology enablement and procurement strategy**. A COO evaluating enterprise software follows the same logic: don't default to the most complex solution, match the tool to the actual capability gap, confirm budget and approval pathways before committing, and train end users thoroughly post-deployment. AT recommendation is clinical procurement intelligence.

> 💡 The ROI of assistive technology is measured in recovered independence — which translates directly to reduced long-term care costs and improved quality-adjusted outcomes, a metric any CFO should recognize.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> An OTA's assistive technology recommendation — low-tech first, matched to client capacity, with funding pathway confirmed — mirrors the COO's technology procurement logic: right tool, right fit, right budget, right training, in that order.

## DOMAIN 8: DOCUMENTATION & REIMBURSEMENT

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **SOAP Note**: Subjective (client report), Objective (measurable data), Assessment (clinical interpretation), Plan (next steps) — most common clinical note format
- **DAP Note**: Data, Assessment, Plan — variation used in some behavioral and community settings
- **Functional goals**: Goals written in measurable, occupation-based terms (NOT "improve ROM" but "client will button shirt independently within 4 weeks")
- **Goal writing formula**: Client + will + do + what + how much + by when + under what conditions
- **Skilled service**: Documentation must prove that the OTA's intervention requires the skill of a trained professional — not something anyone could do
- **Medicare/Medicaid requirements**: Skilled documentation justifying medical necessity is required for reimbursement; missing elements = denied claims
- **Confidentiality (HIPAA)**: All client information is protected; documentation must follow HIPAA guidelines
- **Discontinuation summary**: Written when OT services are ending — summarizes progress, goals met, and home program

**Memorization Framework:**
**SMART-S** — Goal Writing Standard
- **S**pecific (what exactly will the client do)
- **M**easurable (how will you know they did it)
- **A**chievable (realistic for this client)
- **R**elevant (tied to the client's stated occupational goal)
- **T**ime-bound (by when)
- **S**killed (justify why OT expertise is required)

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know the difference between SOAP and DAP notes — and which settings typically use which format
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] Documentation scenarios often present a goal and ask if it's appropriate — look for: functional language, measurable outcome, time frame, and client-centered context
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] Practice rewriting non-functional goals: "Patient will increase ROM" → "Client will reach overhead cabinet independently to retrieve dishes within 6 weeks"

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ Write a SMART-S goal for a client with Parkinson's who wants to return to independent meal preparation. Then write the Objective section of a SOAP note documenting today's cooking session.

---

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain is about the paperwork and proof that clinical work happened, was medically necessary, and deserves payment from insurers. Every treatment session must be documented in a specific way, or the hospital or clinic doesn't get reimbursed — and in the worst case, it becomes a compliance or fraud issue.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **contract compliance and revenue cycle management**. Every billable deliverable needs documentation that proves it was authorized, executed by qualified personnel, met the contracted standard, and achieved a measurable outcome. Sloppy documentation in healthcare is the equivalent of invoicing without a statement of work — it doesn't get paid, and it creates audit risk.

> 💡 In healthcare, documentation IS the product from a reimbursement standpoint — the best clinical care in the world generates zero revenue if it isn't captured in compliant, measurable, occupation-based language.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> An OTA writing a SMART-S goal with functional, measurable, time-bound language is doing exactly what a COO demands from every project deliverable: clear scope, defined success metric, realistic timeline, and evidence that skilled resources — not just any staff — were required to execute.
