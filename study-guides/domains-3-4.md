# Domains 3 & 4: Occupational Performance + Ethics & Professionalism

---

## DOMAIN 3: OCCUPATIONAL PERFORMANCE & ACTIVITY ANALYSIS

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **Occupation**: Any activity a person finds meaningful and engages in routinely — ADLs, IADLs, work, play, leisure, rest/sleep, social participation
- **ADLs (Activities of Daily Living)**: Self-care tasks — bathing, dressing, grooming, eating, toileting, functional mobility
- **IADLs (Instrumental ADLs)**: Complex daily tasks — meal prep, medication management, financial management, driving, home management
- **Activity Analysis**: Breaking down a task into its component steps, demands, and the skills required to perform them
- **Occupational Performance**: The ability to carry out meaningful activities that define one's roles and identity
- **Top-down approach**: Start with what the client wants/needs to do (occupation), then identify barriers
- **Bottom-up approach**: Start with component skills (strength, cognition), then build toward occupation
- **OTPF (Occupational Therapy Practice Framework)**: AOTA's guiding document organizing OT practice domains and process

**Memorization Framework:**
**BADL-REST-PLAY**
- **B**athing, **A**DLs (dressing, grooming), **D**riving/IADLs, **L**eisure
- **R**est and sleep
- **E**ducation
- **S**ocial participation
- **T**ransition/work
- **PLAY** = meaningful occupation across all age groups

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know the difference between ADLs and IADLs cold — and know which OTPF category each belongs to
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] When given a case, identify the occupational performance area being addressed BEFORE identifying the impairment — use the top-down approach
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] Practice doing a complete activity analysis for dressing, meal prep, and transfers — know every performance skill and client factor involved

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ A 72-year-old client post-hip replacement wants to return to gardening. Using the top-down approach, identify the occupation, the performance skills required, and two possible barriers at the body function level.

---

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain is about figuring out what someone needs and wants to do in their daily life — their job, their self-care, their social life — and then breaking down exactly what it takes to do those things. It's the foundation for knowing where to focus treatment.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **job task analysis and role clarity**. Before you optimize a team's performance, you break down what each role actually requires — what decisions, what skills, what outputs. Then you identify where the gaps are. OTAs do the same thing with human function.

> 💡 Organizations that skip role clarity before deploying support resources waste both money and talent — the same way clinicians who skip activity analysis deliver unfocused, ineffective treatment.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> An OTA's activity analysis — breaking a task into its every component before designing an intervention — is the clinical equivalent of a COO's job task analysis: you cannot close a performance gap until you have precisely mapped what the role demands and where the breakdown occurs.

## DOMAIN 4: ETHICS, PROFESSIONALISM & SUPERVISION

### 🎓 [STUDENT] — Layer A: Study Module

**Core Concepts:**
- **AOTA Code of Ethics (2020)**: Six principles governing OT practice — Beneficence, Nonmaleficence, Autonomy, Justice, Veracity, Fidelity
- **Beneficence**: Act in the client's best interest
- **Nonmaleficence**: Do no harm
- **Autonomy**: Respect the client's right to make their own decisions
- **Veracity**: Be truthful in all communications (including documentation)
- **Fidelity**: Honor commitments; maintain professional relationships with integrity
- **OT/OTA Supervisory Relationship**: OTAs work under the supervision of an OT; supervision levels = close, routine, general, minimal
- **Scope of Practice**: OTAs implement treatment plans; OTs evaluate, set goals, and hold final accountability
- **NBCOT**: National Board for Certification in Occupational Therapy — administers the OTA certification exam

**Memorization Framework:**
**BIG JAV** — AOTA's 6 Ethics Principles
- **B**eneficence (do good)
- **I**ntegrity (Fidelity — keep promises)
- **G**iving truth (Veracity — be honest)
- **J**ustice (fair access for all)
- **A**utonomy (client's right to choose)
- **V**oid harm (Nonmaleficence — do no harm)

**High-Yield Exam Tips:**
- 🔵 [Multiple Choice] Know which tasks an OTA CAN and CANNOT do independently — initial evaluations and establishing goals are OT-only functions
- 🟠 [Case Scenario] Ethical dilemma scenarios almost always hinge on Autonomy vs. Beneficence — respect what the client wants even if you think you know better, unless safety is at risk
- 🟢 [Clinical Application] If a scenario asks what to do when you disagree with a supervisor's plan, the answer involves: communicate concern to supervising OT, document, follow chain of command — never act unilaterally

**Self-Assessment Checkpoint:**
> ❓ Your supervising OT is unavailable and a client refuses their prescribed treatment. Which ethical principle is at stake? What should you do, and what should you document?

---

### 💼 [EXEC] — Layer B: Executive Translation

**Plain-Language Summary:**
This domain covers the rules of conduct that govern how OTAs behave — what they can do without supervision, how they handle disagreements, and what ethical principles guide every decision. It also defines the working relationship between OTAs and the OTs who supervise them.

**Business/Leadership Analogy:**
This is **corporate governance and delegation authority**. Every organization has a chain of command, a code of conduct, and clear rules about what decisions require escalation. An OTA who acts beyond their scope without supervisor approval is the clinical equivalent of a manager signing a contract above their authority threshold.

> 💡 Ethics violations in clinical practice carry the same reputational and legal risk as compliance failures in business — and they're almost always preventable with clear role definitions and communication norms.

---

### 🔗 Layer C: Connection Bridge

> The OT/OTA supervisory structure — where scope of practice defines every decision's authority level — is the clinical equivalent of a delegation matrix in corporate governance: both exist to ensure accountability is held at the right level and decisions are never made beyond one's sanctioned authority.
