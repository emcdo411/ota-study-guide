# Domain 4 Prompt: Ethics, Professionalism & Supervision
**Framework Trigger:** `BIG JAV` | **Audience:** OTA Freshman + C-Level Executive (Non-Technical)

---

## 🔵 ADVANCED STUDY PROMPT

> You are a first-year OTA student on your first clinical rotation. Your supervising OT just left the building for a lunch meeting. A patient asks you to start their treatment session independently and change their exercise program because "the old one isn't working." You freeze.
>
> Teach me **Domain 4: Ethics, Professionalism & Supervision** using **BIG JAV** as if I just got my first clinical badge and I'm terrified of making a legal mistake.
>
> Walk me through every principle:
> 1. **Beneficence** — What does "act in the client's best interest" actually mean when it conflicts with what the client wants? Give me a real scenario.
> 2. **Fidelity (Integrity)** — What does it mean to honor your professional commitments? What does it look like when an OTA violates this — even accidentally?
> 3. **Veracity (Truth-telling)** — Why is documentation honesty an ethical requirement, not just a paperwork issue? What are the legal stakes?
> 4. **Justice** — What does fair access to care look like — and what should an OTA do if they believe a patient is being denied services unfairly?
> 5. **Autonomy** — The patient says "I don't want to do therapy today." What is your ethical obligation? When does Autonomy give way to safety concerns?
> 6. **Nonmaleficence** — "Do no harm" sounds obvious. Give me 3 subtle ways an OTA could accidentally harm a patient while trying to help.
>
> Then walk me through the **OT/OTA supervisory relationship**: What can I do independently? What requires OT supervision? What happens if I cross that line?
>
> End with: *"What should I do — step by step — if my supervising OT asks me to do something I believe is unethical or outside my scope of practice?"*

## 💼 C-LEVEL EXECUTIVE TRANSLATION PROMPT

> You are a General Counsel and Chief Compliance Officer at a large health system. Your board is asking about clinical risk, staff authority limits, and what happens when frontline staff overstep their credentialed scope.
>
> Explain **Domain 4: Ethics, Professionalism & Supervision** to me using governance, compliance, and delegation authority language only.
>
> Map it this way:
> 1. **Beneficence** = **Fiduciary duty** — the legal and ethical obligation to act in the stakeholder's best interest, even when inconvenient or costly.
> 2. **Fidelity** = **Contractual integrity** — honoring the terms of your professional role, your scope, and your institutional commitments. What are the breach-of-trust consequences?
> 3. **Veracity** = **Documentation compliance** — every clinical note is a legal record. Falsifying, omitting, or overstating constitutes fraud risk. Same as an executive falsifying board minutes.
> 4. **Justice** = **Equal access and non-discrimination policy** — clinical application of ADA, CMS equity mandates, and organizational DEI obligations.
> 5. **Autonomy** = **Informed consent and stakeholder self-determination** — the client's right to refuse service mirrors a board member's right to dissent. How does the institution protect both the right AND the safety?
> 6. **Nonmaleficence** = **Risk management and harm prevention** — what are the documented exposure categories for clinical harm, and what controls prevent them?
>
> Then translate the **OT/OTA scope structure**: This is your **delegation matrix**. OTs hold final accountability (C-Suite authority). OTAs implement within approved parameters (Director-level execution). Unauthorized scope expansion = unauthorized contract execution.
>
> Close with: *"What is the institutional liability exposure when a frontline staff member acts beyond their credentialed authority — and what governance structures prevent it?"*

| Fidelity (honor professional commitments) | Contractual integrity — honor scope, role, and institutional trust |
| Veracity (honest documentation) | Documentation compliance — every record is a legal artifact |
| Justice (fair access to care) | Equity mandate — ADA, CMS, non-discrimination policy |
| Autonomy (client's right to choose) | Informed consent + stakeholder self-determination rights |
| Nonmaleficence (do no harm) | Risk management — harm prevention and exposure controls |
| OT/OTA supervisory structure | Delegation matrix — authority tiers and escalation thresholds |
| Scope of practice | Credentialed authority limit — unauthorized action = liability event |
| NBCOT certification | Professional licensure = operating authority |
