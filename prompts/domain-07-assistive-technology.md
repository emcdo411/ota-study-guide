# Domain 7 Prompt: Assistive Technology & Adaptive Equipment
**Framework Trigger:** `LAST-F` | **Audience:** OTA Freshman + C-Level Executive (Non-Technical)

---

## 🔵 ADVANCED STUDY PROMPT

> You are a first-year OTA student. Your patient is a 28-year-old man with a C6 spinal cord injury — he has preserved wrist extension but no hand grip. He lives alone in a second-floor apartment and wants to dress himself independently, make his own coffee, and eventually return to work as a graphic designer. His insurance just approved a $4,000 AT budget.
>
> Teach me **Domain 7: Assistive Technology & Adaptive Equipment** using the **LAST-F framework** as if I don't know what AT means and I've never seen a reacher grabber or a power wheelchair in person.
>
> Walk me through each LAST-F step for this patient:
> 1. **Low-Tech First** — Before spending any of that $4,000, what simple, non-electronic tools could help him dress independently? Name at least 4 and explain exactly how each one works.
> 2. **Adaptation of task or environment** — What changes to his apartment layout, his clothing choices, or his morning routine could eliminate barriers without buying anything new?
> 3. **Seating and Positioning** — Why does proper wheelchair positioning have to happen BEFORE you recommend any other AT? What happens to function when positioning is wrong?
> 4. **Technology match** — Now you're looking at higher-tech solutions. What power mobility features, environmental control units, or computer accessibility tools match his goals and preserved function? How do you avoid over-specifying?
> 5. **Funding pathway** — You've identified the right AT. How do you actually get it paid for? Walk me through Medicaid, Medicare, vocational rehabilitation, and private insurance as potential pathways for this patient specifically.
>
> Then: *"Your patient asks why you're not just ordering him the most advanced power chair and smart home system on Day 1. What's your clinical reasoning — and how do you explain it without making him feel like you're holding back?"*

## 💼 C-LEVEL EXECUTIVE TRANSLATION PROMPT

> You are a Chief Technology Officer. Your company just approved a $4M technology modernization budget. Three vendors are pitching you AI, automation, and enterprise SaaS platforms. Your VP of Engineering wants to buy everything. Your CFO wants to buy nothing. You're trying to find the right answer.
>
> Explain **Domain 7: Assistive Technology & Adaptive Equipment** to me using technology procurement, capability enablement, and ROI language only.
>
> Map it this way:
> 1. **Low-Tech First** = **Minimum viable solution** — what's the simplest tool that closes the actual gap? Why do organizations that default to enterprise software for problems that need a checklist waste both money and adoption capacity?
> 2. **Adaptation of task or environment** = **Process re-engineering before tool procurement** — sometimes the workflow is the problem, not the toolset. Audit what's broken before you buy a fix.
> 3. **Seating and Positioning** = **Infrastructure and integration readiness** — you cannot deploy sophisticated technology on top of a broken data architecture or an unprepared workforce. Readiness is prerequisite to ROI.
> 4. **Technology match** = **Capability-to-need fit scoring** — the most advanced tool is not always the right tool. What does the user actually need to accomplish, and what is the minimum technology that reliably enables that outcome?
> 5. **Funding pathway** = **Budget authorization and procurement governance** — identifying the right solution means nothing if you haven't mapped the approval chain, the funding category, and the compliance requirements before you spec it.
>
> Close with: *"What is the organizational cost of a CTO who defaults to maximum technology solutions before auditing minimum viable alternatives — and how does this compare to clinical over-specification in AT recommendation?"*

## 🔗 SIDE-BY-SIDE ANCHOR

| OTA Student Language | C-Level Executive Language |
|---|---|
| Low-Tech AT (reacher, button hook) | Minimum viable solution — simplest tool that closes the gap |
| Environmental modification | Process re-engineering before tool procurement |
| Seating and positioning (prerequisite) | Infrastructure readiness — you can't deploy on a broken foundation |
| Technology match to client capacity | Capability-to-need fit scoring — right tool, not best tool |
| Funding pathway (Medicaid/VR/insurance) | Budget authorization and procurement governance |
| High-Tech AT (power chair, AAC, smart home) | Enterprise platform — powerful, expensive, requires training and maintenance |
| Universal Design | Accessible design by default — builds for the margins, works for everyone |
| AAC device | Communication technology for stakeholders who can't use standard channels |
| OTA trains client on AT | Change management and end-user adoption program |
