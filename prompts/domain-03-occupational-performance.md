# Domain 3 Prompt: Occupational Performance & Activity Analysis
**Framework Trigger:** `BADL-REST-PLAY` + Top-Down Approach | **Audience:** OTA Freshman + C-Level Executive (Non-Technical)

---

## 🔵 ADVANCED STUDY PROMPT

> You are a first-year OTA student. Your instructor just told you: "Before you treat anything, you need to know what the person wants to DO with their life." You wrote that down but you're not sure what it means in practice.
>
> Teach me **Domain 3: Occupational Performance & Activity Analysis** using **BADL-REST-PLAY** as if I've never taken an OT class. I need to be able to DO a real activity analysis by the end of this.
>
> Walk me through:
> 1. What is **"occupation"** in OT? Why do OTs say that cooking, sleeping, and playing with your kids are all "occupations"? Give me 5 examples that would surprise a non-OT person.
> 2. What is the difference between **ADLs** and **IADLs**? Walk me through a 72-year-old woman's morning and identify every ADL and IADL she performs before noon.
> 3. What is **Activity Analysis**? Break down the task of making a peanut butter sandwich into its component steps, the muscles used, the cognitive demands, and the environmental requirements. Do not skip anything.
> 4. What is the **Top-Down Approach**? Why would an OTA start by asking "What do you WANT to do?" instead of "What can your arm do?" What does this tell you about OT's philosophy?
> 5. What is the **OTPF** and why does it matter that AOTA created a framework for organizing all of this?
>
> End with: *"How would a bottom-up approach miss what the top-down approach catches — and what clinical mistake does that difference prevent?"*

---

## 💼 C-LEVEL EXECUTIVE TRANSLATION PROMPT

> You are a Chief People Officer who just inherited a workforce performance problem. Your predecessor gave everyone skills training. Engagement scores still dropped. You don't know why.
>
> Explain **Domain 3: Occupational Performance & Activity Analysis** to me using only organizational design, talent strategy, and leadership language.
>
> Map it this way:
> 1. **Occupation** = **Role identity** — the meaningful work that defines who someone is at work, not just what they produce. Why does losing a role feel like losing identity, and why does this matter for retention?
> 2. **ADLs** = **Core job functions** — the non-negotiable daily outputs that define minimum role performance.
> 3. **IADLs** = **Complex cross-functional responsibilities** — multi-step, judgment-heavy tasks that require coordination, planning, and executive function. These are where high performers differentiate.
> 4. **Activity Analysis** = **Job task analysis and competency mapping** — breaking every role into its granular cognitive, physical, and contextual demands before you design training or accommodation.
> 5. **Top-Down Approach** = **Start with business outcomes, not skills gaps** — ask "What does this person need to accomplish?" before asking "What training do they lack?" Why does this order change your entire intervention strategy?
>
> Close with: *"If HR skips role clarity and task mapping before deploying performance improvement plans, what is the organizational equivalent of treating the wrong injury?"*

## 🔗 SIDE-BY-SIDE ANCHOR

| OTA Student Language | C-Level Executive Language |
|---|---|
| Occupation (meaningful daily activity) | Role identity — meaningful work that defines who someone is |
| ADLs (bathing, dressing, eating) | Core job functions — non-negotiable daily outputs |
| IADLs (meal prep, financials, driving) | Complex cross-functional responsibilities requiring judgment |
| Activity Analysis | Job task analysis + competency mapping |
| Top-Down Approach (start with occupation) | Outcome-first strategy (start with the goal, not the skill gap) |
| Bottom-Up Approach (start with impairment) | Skill-deficit-first approach (often misses the real problem) |
| OTPF | Organizational competency framework (AOTA = SHRM equivalent) |
| Performance skill deficit | Capability gap requiring targeted support |
